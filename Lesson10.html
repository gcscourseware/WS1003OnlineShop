üíª 1. Understanding Platform Analytics

Every e-commerce platform provides a built-in dashboard to monitor essential Key Performance Indicators (KPIs).

Focus on these core numbers in any platform dashboard:

  • Traffic/Sessions: How many times customers visited your store.
  • Average Order Value (AOV): The average amount spent per transaction. Helps you evaluate upselling/cross-selling success.
  • Returning Customer Rate: The percentage of orders placed by customers who have previously bought from you. A key indicator of loyalty.
  • Revenue by Channel: Which platform (your website, Instagram Shop, Shopee) drives the most sales.

When selling directly on social media, track the following beyond standard platform metrics:

  1. Click-Through Rate (CTR): The percentage of people who clicked your product link after viewing your ad/post.
  2. Engagement Rate: Total likes, comments, and shares divided by total impressions. High engagement often precedes high sales.
  3. Cost Per Acquisition (CPA): How much money it costs you to acquire one paying customer through an ad campaign.

üéØ 2. Measuring Sales Conversion Rates

Conversion Rate is the most important metric for determining how effective your website or social shop is at turning visitors into buyers. [Image of sales funnel conversion stages]

$$\text{Conversion Rate} = \frac{\text{Number of Orders}}{\text{Number of Sessions}} \times 100$$

A good e-commerce conversion rate is typically between 1% and 3%, but this varies widely by industry and traffic source. Social commerce traffic often converts lower than search traffic.

Instead of just the final sales rate, look at each step:

  • Visitor to Cart: The drop-off rate here indicates issues with product presentation (images, descriptions) or pricing.
  • Cart to Checkout: Low conversion here indicates problems with shipping costs, confusing site navigation, or lack of trust signals.
  • Checkout to Purchase (Payment): High drop-off here points to issues with accepted payment methods or unexpected fees/taxes.

üí° 3. Making Data-Driven Improvements

Data should lead directly to testing and optimization, not just observation.

Match the metric to the improvement action:

  • Problem: Low AOV?
    **Action:** Introduce bundles, offer free shipping above a higher threshold, or implement a "Frequently Bought Together" section.
  • Problem: Low Cart-to-Checkout?
    **Action:** Add trust badges (payment logos, security seals) near the checkout button or simplify the checkout form to a single page.
  • Problem: High CPA (Cost Per Acquisition)?
    **Action:** Refine ad targeting to a more specific audience or improve the landing page experience to increase its quality score.

Never change everything at once. Test one element at a time (e.g., button color, product description, image gallery layout) to isolate the impact.

Hypothesis Structure: "If I change [ELEMENT], then [METRIC] will improve by [AMOUNT] because [REASON]." (e.g., "If I change the Buy button color to orange, conversion will improve by 10% because orange draws more attention.")
